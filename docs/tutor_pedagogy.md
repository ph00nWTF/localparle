## 1. CEFR Vocabulary Sizes (French-specific)

**Source:** Milton, J. & Alexiou, T. (2009). *Vocabulary size and the Common European
Framework of Reference for Languages.* In Richards, B. et al. (Eds.), *Vocabulary Studies
in First and Second Language Acquisition.* Palgrave Macmillan.

Milton & Alexiou measured vocabulary size against CEFR level across 500+ learners of
English, Greek, and French. French-specific findings:

| Level | Vocabulary size (French learners) |
|---|---|
| A2 | ~1,650 words |
| B1 | ~2,422 words |
| B2 | ~2,630 words |
| C1 | ~3,212 words |
| C2 | ~3,525 words |

> "Progressively higher vocabulary scores are associated with progressively higher levels
> in the CEFR hierarchy... the differences between the mean scores at each CEFR level are
> statistically significant." (Milton & Alexiou 2009, pp. 200–204, via Semantic Scholar)

**Caution:** Milton & Alexiou note their samples are comparatively small. These figures
are a starting point, not hard ceilings.

**Approximate A1 target (Oxford/consensus):** ~500 active words. Not from Milton &
Alexiou directly — A1 learners were not well-represented in their sample.

**Links:**
- [Semantic Scholar entry](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Vocabulary-Size-and-the-Common-European-Framework-Milton-Alexiou/abe5103726bbdc2505a92ddf00d078af4cfa9515)

## 2. CEFR Grammar Targets by Level

**Source:** Council of Europe. *Common European Framework of Reference for Languages:
Learning, Teaching, Assessment.* Cambridge University Press, 2001.
[Official CEFR page](https://www.coe.int/en/web/common-european-framework-reference-languages)

Grammar targets below are derived from the CEFR can-do descriptors and corroborated by
[KwizIQ French grammar by CEFR level](https://french.kwiziq.com/revision/grammar/by-cefr-level).

| Level | Core grammar targets |
|---|---|
| A1 | Present tense (être, avoir, aller, faire, -ER verbs), subject pronouns, articles (le/la/un/une), negation (ne…pas), il y a |
| A2 | Passé composé intro, adjective agreement, possessives, relative pronouns (qui/que), comparatives |
| B1 | Imparfait, passé composé vs. imparfait distinction, conditional present, subjunctive intro |
| B2 | Pluperfect, subjunctive (extensive), passive voice, complex conditionals |
| C1 | All tenses, register control, nuanced grammar, idiomatic constructions |
| C2 | Near-native accuracy across all registers, stylistic variation |

## 3. Corrective Feedback Types

**Source:** Lyster, R. & Ranta, L. (1997). Corrective feedback and learner uptake:
Negotiation of form in communicative classrooms. *Studies in Second Language Acquisition,
19*(1), 37–66.
[Cambridge Core (paywalled)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/studies-in-second-language-acquisition/article/abs/corrective-feedback-and-learner-uptake/59229F0CA2F085F5F5016FB4674877BF)
[ERIC abstract (free)](https://eric.ed.gov/?id=EJ539354)

Lyster & Ranta analyzed **18.3 hours** of classroom interaction in four French immersion
classes at the primary level (14 subject-matter lessons + 13 French language arts lessons).

**Verified from published abstract (Cambridge Core) and primary source tables:**
> "The findings indicate an overwhelming tendency for teachers to use recasts in spite of
> the latter's ineffectiveness at eliciting student-generated repair. Four other feedback
> types—elicitation, metalinguistic feedback, clarification requests, and repetition—lead
> to student-generated repair more successfully and are thus able to initiate what the
> authors characterize as the negotiation of form."

**Verified from primary source tables (Lyster & Ranta 1997, pp. 46–54):**

Feedback type frequency across 686 teacher feedback turns:

| Type | n | % of feedback |
|---|---|---|
| Recast | 375 | 55% |
| Elicitation | 94 | 14% |
| Clarification request | 73 | 11% |
| Metalinguistic feedback | 58 | 8% |
| Explicit correction | 50 | 7% |
| Repetition | 36 | 5% |

Uptake by feedback type:

| Type | Repair | Needs repair | No uptake | Student-generated repair |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Recast (n=375) | 66 (18%) | 49 (13%) | 260 (69%) | **0 (0%)** |
| Elicitation (n=94) | 43 (46%) | 51 (54%) | 0 | **43 (46%)** |
| Clarification (n=73) | 20 (28%) | 44 (60%) | 9 (12%) | **20 (27%)** |
| Metalinguistic (n=58) | 26 (45%) | 24 (41%) | 8 (14%) | **26 (45%)** |
| Explicit correction (n=50) | 18 (36%) | 7 (14%) | 25 (50%) | **0 (0%)** |
| Repetition (n=36) | 11 (31%) | 17 (47%) | 8 (22%) | **11 (31%)** |

Key finding: Recasts produced **zero** student-generated repairs. Elicitation,
metalinguistic feedback, clarification, and repetition all produced student-generated
repair. Explicit correction, like recast, produced zero — the teacher corrected, not
the learner.

**Implementation rule:** Default correction strategy is **elicitation** (not recast) at
all levels. Use explicit correction only when elicitation fails twice on the same error.
Never use recast as a primary correction strategy.

## 4. Krashen's Input Hypothesis

**Source:** Krashen, S. D. (1982). *Principles and Practice in Second Language
Acquisition.* Oxford: Pergamon Press.
Krashen, S. D. (1985). *The Input Hypothesis.* Harrow: Longman.

Both books are not open access. The following quote is from a verified secondary source
(sk.com.br, a Krashen summary maintained by Roberto Schütz):

> "'comprehensible input' is the crucial and necessary ingredient for the acquisition of
> language."

**Verified directly from primary source (Krashen 1982, pp. 20–21):**

> "a necessary (but not sufficient) condition to move from stage i to stage i + 1 is that
> the acquirer understand input that contains i + 1, where 'understand' means that the
> acquirer is focussed on the meaning and not the form of the message."

> "(2) We acquire by understanding language that contains structure a little beyond our
> current level of competence (i + 1). This is done with the help of context or
> extra-linguistic information."

> "(3) When communication is successful, when the input is understood and there is enough
> of it, i + 1 will be provided automatically."

**Implementation rule:** Mistral's responses must be constructed at the learner's current
CEFR level + small increment — not at native-speaker speed or complexity.

**Links:**
- [Wikipedia: Input Hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Input_hypothesis)
- [sk.com.br Krashen summary](https://www.sk.com.br/sk-krash-english.html)
- [PMC critique of Krashen (open access)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12577063/)

## 5. Spaced Repetition

### Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve
**Source:** Ebbinghaus, H. (1885/1913). *Memory: A Contribution to Experimental
Psychology.* New York: Teachers College, Columbia University.
Public domain — [Wikipedia summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forgetting_curve)

Core finding: without review, ~50% of new material is forgotten within 30 minutes and
70–80% within 24 hours.

### Optimal Spacing Intervals
**Source:** Cepeda, N. J., Vul, E., Rohrer, D., Wixted, J. T., & Pashler, H. (2008).
Spacing effects in learning: A temporal ridgeline of optimal retention. *Psychological
Science, 19*(11), 1095–1102.
[Author's page (free)](https://www.yorku.ca/ncepeda/publications/CVRWP2008.html)
[PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19076480/)

Study: 1,350+ individuals, gaps up to 3.5 months, final tests up to 1 year later.

**Verified direct quote:**
> "The optimum gap value was about 20% of the test delay for delays of a few weeks,
> falling to about 5% when delay was one year."

**Practical intervals derived from this (consistent with Duolingo research):**

| Review # | Days since last review |
|---|---|
| 1st | 1 day |
| 2nd | 3 days |
| 3rd | 7 days |
| 4th | 21 days |
| 5th+ | Multiply by ~2–2.5× each time |

### SM-2 Algorithm
**Source:** Woźniak, P. (1987). *SuperMemo 2 Algorithm.*
[Original documentation (free)](https://www.supermemo.com/en/blog/application-of-a-computer-to-improve-the-results-obtained-in-working-with-the-supermemo-method)

**Verified direct quotes from original source:**

Interval formula:
```
I(1) := 1
I(2) := 6
for n > 2: I(n) := I(n-1) * EF
```

Easiness factor update:
```
EF' := EF + (0.1 - (5 - q) * (0.08 + (5 - q) * 0.02))
```
Minimum EF: 1.3

Quality scale:
- `5` — "perfect response"
- `4` — "correct response after a hesitation"
- `3` — "correct response recalled with serious difficulty"
- `2` — "incorrect response; where the correct one seemed easy to recall"
- `1` — "incorrect response; the correct one remembered"
- `0` — "complete blackout"

If quality < 3: reset interval to 1 day.

## 6. English-Speaker Error Patterns in French

**Source:** Compiled from:
- [FrenchLearner: French False Cognates](https://www.frenchlearner.com/vocabulary/false-cognates/)
- [FrenchLearner: Noun Gender Rules](https://www.frenchlearner.com/grammar/noun-gender-rules/)
- [CopyCat Cafe: Gender patterns (80% rule)](https://copycatcafe.com/blog/french-nouns-gender)
- [Clozemaster: Common mistakes English speakers make](https://www.clozemaster.com/blog/common-mistakes-english-speakers-make-french/)

These are practitioner/pedagogical sources, not primary research. No verified primary
research paper on English→French error frequencies was found in this pass.

**Priority error types to track in state machine:**

1. **Noun gender** — no reliable rule covers all cases; must be memorized with the article.
   Always teach `la maison`, never `maison`. The 80% pattern rule covers endings like
   -ment (masc), -ion (fem), -té (fem), -age (masc) but has common exceptions.

2. **Adjective agreement** — English has no equivalent; systematic errors expected at A1–B1.

3. **False cognates** — test frequently. High-priority examples:
   - `embarrassé` = confused/troubled (≠ embarrassed)
   - `actuellement` = currently (≠ actually)
   - `librairie` = bookstore (≠ library)
   - `déception` = disappointment (≠ deception)
   - `demander` = to ask (≠ to demand)

4. **être vs. avoir as auxiliary** — aller, venir, partir, arriver, naître, mourir +
   reflexive verbs take être; most others take avoir.

5. **Prepositions for destinations** — `à` (cities), `en` (feminine countries/continents),
   `au` (masculine countries), `aux` (plural countries).

## 7. VanPatten's Processing Instruction

**Source:** VanPatten, B. & Cadierno, T. (1993). Input processing and second language
acquisition: A role for instruction. *The Modern Language Journal, 77*(1), 45–57.
JSTOR stable URL: https://www.jstor.org/stable/329557

### The Input Processing Model

VanPatten proposes that SLA consists of three distinct process sets:

> "input → intake → developing system → output"
>
> "Input processing is concerned with the first set of processes; that is, the conversion
> of input to intake... input processing, as used here, involves those strategies and
> mechanisms that promote form-meaning connections during comprehension."
> (p. 46)

Critically, input, intake, and output are not the same thing. Comprehension does not
automatically produce acquisition:

> "sufficient arguments exist that comprehension does not necessarily lead to
> acquisition." (p. 46)

### The First Noun / Word Order Strategy

> "Evidence from child L1, and both child and adult L2 studies, shows that early and
> intermediate learners assign agent status to the first noun(phrase) of a string and
> object status to the second noun(phrase)." (p. 47)

For French: a learner applying this strategy to "Le chien est mordu par l'homme" will
misread it as "the dog bites the man." The strategy works for simple SVO sentences but
fails for passives, object-fronted constructions, and clitic pronouns.

### Traditional vs. Processing Instruction

> **Table I — Summary of Traditional vs. Processing Instruction**
>
> | | Traditional | Processing |
> |---|---|---|
> | | Paradigmatic | Non-paradigmatic |
> | | Focus on output | Focus on input |
> | | Some focus on meaning | Meaning always in focus |

Traditional instruction: explain a grammar rule → drill output production.
Processing instruction: teach learners to correctly interpret input strings (no output
production required during instruction).

> "We should stress here that at no point did processing instruction involve the
> production of the pronoun forms by the learners." (p. 48–49)

### Results

**Interpretation task** (can the learner correctly understand the sentence?):

> **Table II — Mean Raw Scores for Interpretation Task** (max = 10)
>
> | | No instruction | Traditional | Processing |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Pretest | 1.9 | 2.6 | 2.5 |
> | Post-test 1 | 4.5 | 4.6 | **9.4** |
> | Post-test 2 | 5.6 | 3.9 | **7.5** |
> | Post-test 3 | 4.2 | 6.0 | **8.8** |

> "processing instruction was superior to the other two instructional types vis-a-vis the
> interpretation test." (p. 51)

> "No significant difference between traditional and no instruction (p = .96)." (p. 51)

**Production task** (can the learner correctly produce the target form?):

> **Table IV — Mean Raw Scores for Production Task** (max = 10)
>
> | | No instruction | Traditional | Processing |
> |---|---|---|---|
> | Pretest | 2.1 | 3.6 | 1.8 |
> | Post-test 1 | 4.2 | 9.3 | **8.3** |
> | Post-test 2 | 4.2 | 7.9 | **8.0** |
> | Post-test 3 | 4.2 | 8.3 | **8.3** |

> "the analysis did not yield any significant difference between processing instruction
> and traditional instruction." (p. 51)

### Conclusion (verbatim)

> "Learners who receive instruction that attempts to alter input processing receive a
> double bonus: better processing of input and knowledge that is apparently also
> available for production." (p. 54)

### Implementation rule

Processing instruction improves both comprehension AND production; traditional
output-drill instruction improves production but does NOT improve comprehension.
For the tutor state machine:

- Design activities where the learner must **interpret** input correctly before being
  asked to produce — not the reverse
- When teaching a new structure, expose the learner to it in context (structured input)
  before requiring them to produce it
- Explicitly counter the First Noun Strategy for French passives, object pronouns, and
  any non-SVO construction
